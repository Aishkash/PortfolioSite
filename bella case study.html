1. Introduction

How Can a Wellness Technology Company Play It Smart?

Step 1: Ask

Background

Bellabeat is a high-tech company that manufactures health-focused smart products. They offer various smart devices that collect data on activity, sleep, stress, and reproductive health to empower women with insights about their well-being and habits.

The main goal of this case study is to analyze smart device fitness data and determine how it could help unlock new growth opportunities for Bellabeat. We will focus specifically on one of Bellabeat’s flagship products: the Bellabeat App.

The Bellabeat app provides users with health data related to their activity, sleep, stress, menstrual cycle, and mindfulness habits. This data enables users to better understand their current behaviors and make healthier decisions. The app integrates seamlessly with Bellabeat’s suite of smart wellness devices.

Key Stakeholders

  • Urška Sršen — Bellabeat’s co-founder and Chief Creative Officer
  • Sando Mur — Bellabeat’s co-founder and Chief Technology Officer
  • Marketing Analytics Team — responsible for discovering growth opportunities through data

Step 2: Prepare

Dataset Used

The dataset used for this case study is FitBit Fitness Tracker Data. It is hosted on Kaggle and was made available by Mobius. The data was generated by respondents to a distributed survey via Amazon Mechanical Turk between March 12, 2016 and May 12, 2016.

Accessibility and Privacy of Data

The dataset is licensed under CC0: Public Domain, meaning the creator has waived all rights to the work worldwide under copyright law. This allows anyone to copy, modify, distribute, or perform the work — even for commercial purposes — without requesting permission.

Data Organization and Verification

The dataset is a collection of 18 CSV files, including 15 in long format and 3 in wide format. It contains diverse information such as activity metrics, calories burned, sleep records, metabolic equivalent tasks (METs), heart rate, and step counts — recorded across different time intervals (seconds, minutes, hours, and days).

Data Limitations

While the dataset provides valuable insights, several limitations could affect the accuracy and generalizability of the analysis:

  • Missing demographic information — the dataset lacks details on users’ age, gender, or lifestyle context.
  • Small sample size — only a limited number of respondents contributed to the dataset.
  • Short data collection period — the dataset covers a very brief time window (approximately two months).

Category Creation¶

Now, let's create custom categories for the users based on their level of physical activity and device usage.

Physical Activity Categories¶

We'll define physical activity levels using the following criteria:

  • Sedentary: Less than 6,000 daily steps on average
  • Active: Between 6,000 and 12,000 daily steps on average
  • Very Active: More than 12,000 daily steps on average

Device Usage Categories¶

We’ll use these criteria for device usage (though this dataset doesn’t yet include hourly data):

  • Low Use: Less than 8 hours of use per day
  • Normal Use: Between 8 and 16 hours of use per day
  • High Use: More than 16 hours of use per day

For this dataset, we’ll start by creating only the activity_level category.
The device_usage category will be created later when we analyze an hourly dataset.

id_grp = df.groupby(['id'])

id_avg_step = id_grp['total_steps'].mean().sort_values(ascending=False)

# After that, I turn the results into a dataframe
id_avg_step = id_avg_step.to_frame()

# I want to create a new column which tells in which category each user fits into, depending on the average amount of steps
conditions = [
    (id_avg_step <=6000),
    (id_avg_step > 6000) & (id_avg_step < 12000),
    (id_avg_step >= 12000)
] # These are the conditions

values = ['sedentary','active','very_active'] # And here are the name of the values

# I create a column with the numpy function, np.select to asign each id a category
id_avg_step['activity_level'] = np.select(conditions,values)

# I store the results in a variable to use it in the next step
id_activity_level = id_avg_step['activity_level']

# I use a list comprehension to create the column in our original dataset.
# With this list comprehension I retrieve the categories where the index match the id column
df['activity_level'] = [id_activity_level[c] for c in df['id']]

Step 6: Act¶

After analyzing the FitBit Fitness Tracker data, we’ve identified several insights that could help shape Bellabeat’s marketing strategy.

A Multipurpose Device¶

Bellabeat can communicate to users that their products are not just meant for sports or exercise-related activities.
The data shows that many users wear their fitness trackers more often on weekends than weekdays — this may suggest they associate the device primarily with workouts or leisure walks.

Bellabeat can reposition its products as everyday wellness companions that support users throughout their entire day — whether they’re working, commuting, or doing household chores.
By emphasizing overall health tracking and lifestyle improvement, Bellabeat can attract women from diverse backgrounds who care about maintaining a balanced and healthy lifestyle.

Rewards and Reminders¶

Bellabeat could integrate motivational features within the Bellabeat app or devices, such as rewards, challenges, and reminders, to encourage consistent healthy habits.

Some ideas include:

  • Setting daily or weekly goals, like achieving 7,500 steps per day, burning a target number of calories, or maintaining 8 hours of sleep.
  • Offering virtual medals, badges, or discounts for users who consistently meet these goals.
  • Displaying a leaderboard to inspire friendly competition among users.
  • Sending personalized notifications when users fall behind on their goals, along with helpful recommendations to improve their activity, sleep, or overall health.

These features would not only keep users engaged but also help build a sense of motivation, accountability, and community around Bellabeat’s products.
